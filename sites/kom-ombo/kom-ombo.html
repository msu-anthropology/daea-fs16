Kom Ombo

First Dynasty to Coptic Period

Joanna Carroll

Introduction

Starting in the First Dynasty spanning to the Coptic period is the timeframe for the archaeological site of Kom Ombo. This site is located in Upper Egypt, directly off of the Nile River to the East, where it served as a trade route. The a large area of the site consists of the Kom Ombo plain, but a major component of the site at Kom Ombo is the Kom Ombo Temple. The Kom Ombo Temple is unique in that the temple is built for two Gods, Horus and Sobek. The temple has separate spaces dedicated to both gods within the same complex. Significant finds and artifacts found at this site ranged from the early depictions of surgical tools on temple walls to numerous mummified crocodiles found within the temple structure. Major excavations for the site occurred at the earliest in 1893 by Jacques de Morgan who focused on the Kom Ombo Temple, and in the 1920s there were excavations led by Edmund Vignard at sites on the Kom Ombo plain. Vignard’s discoveries led to more excavations in the 1960’s on the Kom Ombo plain to help efforts to preserve sites on the plain that have had pressures from the pressing urbanization in the area. In present day Egypt, the Temple of Kom Ombo has remained a popular tourist stop along the Nile River.


Location, Geography, and Geology

The site of Kom Ombo is located in Upper Egypt, approximately 30 miles south of Edfu and 25 miles north of Aswan. Located directly on the east side of the Nile River, the site of Kom Ombo was an ideal location for ease of access to water and provided trade routes into areas such as Nubia. The timespan of Kom Ombo ranged from the archaeological evidence found dating back to the First Dynasty to more recent additions to the Kom Ombo Temple that have Roman influences (Smith 1966). Image 1 shows a present day picture of the Kom Ombo Temple from the Nile River.


The site of Kom Ombo is located in part of the Eastern Desert, and is also part of the Garara graben. The Ganara graben is a depressed block of land located in between Kom Ombo and the Red Sea. The site is part of a stretch of the Nile River that consists mostly of sandstone, and the sandstone works as a boundary for the site. Specifically, the Kom Ombo plain is contained by sandstone cliffs to the north at Gebel El-Silsila, and bounded by sandstone to the south. Thus, the materials from which structures at the site exist consist of the sandstone similar to what is found nearby. The point in the Nile River where the site of Kom Ombo is located ranges in width from 350 meters at the narrowest portion, but can reach widths near 700 meters nearby. The plain is about 20 meters above the level of the Nile River, and totaling to about 90 meters above sea level (Wendorf 1976).


The Kom Ombo plain has a distinct strata of rocks covered with different layers of sand and silt. The strata at the site consists of rock dated to the Eocene and Cretaceous time periods. On the north end of the Kom Ombo plain, sea urchins from the Eocene time period can even be found within the strata. On the southern edge of the plain, layers of rock consist of limestone (Hume 1907).

Site Description

The site of Kom Ombo consists of two major components: the Kom Ombo Temple and the Kom Ombo plain. This plain does not have any significant, physical features. However, this plain is thought to have been used to house herded animals because of the flat, fertile land and easy access to water. There are even large amounts of existing petroglyphs found at on the plain that depict numerous animals, such as bovids, gazelles and fish. Scattered sites have also been found on the Kom Ombo plain with evidence of animal bones, shells, blades, and grinding stones being a majority of the archaeological finds. The Kom Ombo plain is bounded by large areas of sandstone. This sandstone contributed to a majority of the construction materials of the temple within the city (Smith 1966).


A major component of the site at Kom Ombo consists of the Temple of Kom Ombo. The temple structure is unique due to the fact that the structure consists of two independent temples that exist within one conjoined temple structure. Each independent portion is either dedicated to the god Horus or god Sobek. This results in the temple complex having two entrances and two, separate areas to honor both gods. On the south east side of the temple, Sobek is worshiped. Sobeck is the ancient god of the crocodiles. Sobek was thought to have controlled the Nile River and the fertile land that goes along with it. The god Horus, who is worshiped on the north west of the temple, was part hawk that was considered to be the god of the sky. Both areas of the temple are decorated with depictions of both gods (Hill 2010). Images of both Horus and Sobek can be seen at the site in Image 2.


The temple structure at Kom Ombo had duplicate chambers for both gods. There were two offering halls, along with two entrances and two sanctuaries for each god. The temple also had a small birth house as part of the temple complex with references to the god Hathor. Additionally, the Temple of Kom Ombo also had additions made to it during the Coptic period. This included pylons and walls with depictions referencing Roman influence, and a small chapel area (Hill 2010).


Beyond the Kom Ombo temple itself, the artifacts and significant finds excavated from the site. This included numerous grinding stones and animal bones found on the Kom Ombo plain. The large quantities of petroglyphs of animals found on the Kom Ombo plain also provided a level of diversity and understanding to the settlements in the area (Smith 1966). The temple ground at Kom Ombo was found to have mummified animal bodies including numerous crocodiles, falcons, and serpents. There is also a nilometer within the temple complex (Hill 2010). Image 3 shows mummified animals that were found at the temple of Kom Ombo. There is a diversity of hieroglyphs on the walls and pylons of the temple that show the diversity from both the Roman and Egyptian backgrounds (Hill 2010).


Present day, much of the Temple of Kom Ombo is in a state of ruin. Most of the temple structure is still standing, however, areas such as pylons in the temple enclosure, stairways, and artifacts are either nonexistent or left in ruin. Currently, the Kom Ombo area has a large Nubian population that was relocated here due to the formation of Lake Nasser (Hill 2010).


Excavations and Significance of Excavation Results

The excavations at Kom Ombo have been beneficial in providing information about the diverse and unique findings of the site in Egypt. One of the earliest known excavations at the temple of Kom Ombo was carried out by Jacques de Morgan in 1893. Jacques de Morgan was a French archaeologist whose excavation was responsible for clearing out much of the debris at the temple. This excavation also provided some early, minor work for preservation of the temple (Bard 1999).


Another significant excavation for Kom Ombo took place in the 1920’s, led by Edmund Vignard. These excavations were significant in that numerous petroglyphs of animals, grinding stones, blades, and deposits of animal bones were found on the Kom Ombo plain. Some of the items were even found scattered over the plain. It was during these excavations that Edmund Vignard was able to develop the Silibian Sequence based off of the early dated grinding stones at the plain (Wendrich 2010).


In the 1960’s, further effort for excavations on the Kom Ombo plain were conducted based off of the some of the significant findings of Edmund Vignard, and the early contexts of the artifacts that were found. These efforts were instigated by pressure of installing irrigation ditches in the Kom Ombo plain due to the increasing population in the area. The recent excavations specifically at the Kom Ombo plain or the Kom Ombo temple decreased due to the increase in population, urbanization, and tourism in the area (Wendrich 2010).

Conclusion

Kom Ombo is a significant archaeological site along the Nile River for both its diversity and uniqueness in the archaeology of Egypt. The Kom Ombo temple is unlike many other temples in the area due to fact that the temple is dedicated to multiple gods. Having a conjoined temple complex is unique compared to other temples. The temple has two entrances, two areas to supply offerings, and two specific sanctuaries for both the god Horus and the god Sobek. Beyond the temple, the Kom Ombo plain excavations lead to diverse site excavations that date artifacts to some of the earliest in the area. This ultimately lead an effort in the 1960’s to excavate the plain due to pressures of urbanization. In present day Egypt, due to the easy access of the site right off of the Nile River, the area has become a popular tourist spot and home to a growing population.
