Kom El Hisn

OLD KINGDOM

Katie Doyen

Introduction

Kom el-Hisn, or the “Hill of the Fort” translated in Arabic, is an archaeological site located in Egypt’s Western Delta. It is thought to be the capital of the Third Nome of ancient Egypt, also known as the “Estate of Cattle”. This excavation found archaeological evidence that the site was occupied during the Old Kingdom, with a large community in the 4th – 6th Dynasties, and into the Middle Kingdom. This site has many large discoveries such as a large mud-brick temple, four statues of Ramesses II, the Tomb of Khesu-war, and over one thousand burials (Bard, 1999). Also found were the remains of cattle and an even higher amount of cattle dung used to fertilize plants. Since there was such a large amount of dung, this site might have been used to raise cattle that were later moved and consumed in larger cities.

Flinders Petrie originally discovered this site in 1881, but his student Francis Ll. Griffith was the one to record information in 1885 and 1887 (Kirby, 1996). Griffith discovered the mud-brick temple walls that were dedicated to Sekhmet and Hathor. The next major excavation occurred in the 1940s by an Egyptian team. This happened when almost 1,000 burials were discovered due to heavy rainfall that exposed mud-brick tombs (Kirby, 1996). Most of the site has now turned into agricultural fields due to increasing urbanization of Egypt (Bard, 1999).


Introduction to Location

Kom el-Hisn is in Lower Egypt’s Western Delta. It is about 90km from the current coastline of the Nile, but may have been as close as 50km during the Old Kingdom (Bard, 1999). It is located near the desert, the Delta, the Libyan frontier, and the Mediterranean making it diverse for early socio-political systems (Wenke, 1988). It is west of the current city Tanta and southeast of the current city Damanhur. Kom el-Hisn was the capital of the Third Nome during the Old Kingdom (Wenke, 1988). Kom el-Hisn was more forested than it looks like today because of recent and continuous agricultural expansion. The soil is sandy and not the most productive for agriculture, so cow dung was used to fertilize the soil for fodder crops, such as clovers, and other weeds (Bard, 1999). It is only a few meters above sea level; therefore Delta flooding affects the area greatly. The buildings are constructed of mud-brick walls and the statues were made of basalt.


Description of Site

Kom el-Hisn is a large site about 1 mile by 1/3 mile. The first discoveries were made of the Ramesses II statues. There were four statues in total near a thick pylon on the eastern end of the site (Cagle, 2001). At the northern end of the site, there were almost one thousand burials excavated. There is also the large limestone tomb of Khesu-war. Most of the burials were from the latter part of the First Intermediate period, but some Middle Kingdom and New Kingdom burials were also found (Cagle, 2001). This was realized because of the differences in the pottery that was found within the graves. Lastly, there is a temple for the goddesses Hathor and Sekhmet. Inscriptions of the temple walls reveal Hathor as the main deity of the time (Bard, 1999).














Excavations - Past and Present

The first discovery of Kom el-Hisn happened when Flinders Petrie discovered the statue of Ramesses II. His student, Griffith, returned and found a rectangular mud-brick temple with a thick pylon and four statues of Ramesses II within it (Kirby, 1996). These are not visible in today’s time. In 1910, a local antiquarian found the tomb of Khesu-war on the southwest corner of the site. C. Edgar found a basalt head of a twelfth dynasty king wearing a white crown and many cow bones at the north end of the site (Kirby, 1996). Next, the Egyptian team of Hamad, el-Amir, and Farid excavated the thousand burials in the 1940s after an antiquities director saw mud-brick walls after a heavy rainfall (Kirby, 1996). They dug up six test pits in order to find all of the walls to the Sekhmet and Hathir temple that Griffith originally spotted.

In more current research, there were three excavations during 1984, 1986, and 1988 focusing on the southern portion of the site led by Robert J. Wenke (Cagle, 2001). This was thought as the main areas of habitation with most of the site being covered with ceramics. In 1984, they randomly selected test pits and trenches (Cagle, 2001). After looking at these results, in 1986 they decided to do a stratified random sample to clarify the structure of the site (Cagle, 2001). Finally, they performed one more excavation in 1988 and found more buildings and rooms. The artifacts discovered in this area of occupation were all used in basic household activities. Some artifacts that were found were bread molds, shallow bowls, jars, and ground stone tools (Cagle, 2001). Radio carbon dating found that most of these items were used in the 5th and 6th dynasties with some relating all the way to the Middle Kingdom (Wenke, 1988). There were also faunal remains of sheep, goats, pigs, cattle, and fish (Cagle, 2001). The site is lacking in cattle bones with very few being found for the Old Kingdom time period.


Significant Results

One of the most important discoveries to this site was the comparison of cattle bones to the cattle dung that was used as fertilizer for the plants and as a fuel source for fires. Since there were not many bones, the site may have consisted of cattle in pens instead of free-range. The bones that were found were of young animals not yet reaching maturity. This suggests that Kom el-Hisn was used as a cattle-farming community. These cattle were bred and shipped off to larger cities to be slaughtered and eaten. Kom el-Hisn, as a place for cattle breeding, is further proven by looking at the floral remains of cereal straw, fodder plants, and field weeds (Wenke, 1988). By looking at the preserved dung that was burned for cooking, it is clear there are preserved plants inside. Due to the regularity of these plants in the dung cakes, it shows that the cattle were fed in a pen instead of being left open to graze. This adds significance to the making of Temple of Hathor in this area because this goddess was often portrayed with cow features.

The thousands of graves that were discovered at Kom el-Hisn have importance as well because this site was likely a location that people habitated. Instead of it just being the site of a temple that was only visited and few lived there, Kom el-Hisn was likely inhabited over a long period of time. All of this is still questionable because archaeologists are unsure if this site was able to be self-sufficient. Since cattle was shipped out of the area and most flora remains were used to feed the cattle, it would have been difficult for a large amount of people to sustain themselves. Self-sufficiency was typically higher than this in the Old Kingdom, so this site is unusual for its time period (Cagle, 2001).

Finally, the significance of the excavations of Kom el-Hisn are that it was a powerful political site in the Old Kingdom government. It would have had strong ties to the central government because of all the trading of the cattle (Cagle, 2001). Also, it was the capital of the Third Nome, which would have made it have high economic importance. Since they did not produce many of their own goods, they would have had to trade with areas all over in order to survive. Therefore, Kom el-Hisn was an important political powerhouse in ancient Egypt.
